2.1

Learning a Class from Examples

This section sets up the foundational problem of supervised learning: we want a machine to learn what constitutes a class (concept) by observing labelled examples, and then correctly identify new unseen instances as members or non-members of that class.

The Setup: What Are We Learning?

Imagine we want a program to recognise family sedans from car images. A human domain expert provides some positive examples (cars that are family sedans) and some negative examples (cars that are not). The learner must find a rule — a hypothesis — that correctly separates the two groups and then generalize to unseen cars.

✦ Core Definition
A class (or concept) $C$ is a subset of the input space $\mathcal{X}$. We are given a training sample $\mathcal{X}$ of $N$ labeled points, and we want to learn a hypothesis $h \in \mathcal{H}$ that approximates $C$. A hypothesis is a function $h: \mathcal{X} \to \{0, 1\}$ (positive/negative).

Concrete Example — Family Sedan Recognition

The textbook example uses two input features for a car:

  • $x_1$ — Price (in thousands of USD)
  • $x_2$ — Engine power (in horsepower)

We observe positive examples (family sedan = ✦) and negative examples (not sedan = ✗) and want to find a region in this two-dimensional space that contains all and only the family sedans.

The Hypothesis Space H

The learner does not know the true concept $C$. Instead, it searches through a predefined hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}$ — the set of all candidate hypotheses the algorithm considers. The choice of $\mathcal{H}$ encodes our inductive bias: our prior belief about what kind of rules are plausible.

📐 Axis-Parallel Rectangles

In the textbook's car example, hypotheses are axis-parallel rectangles in the $(x_1, x_2)$ plane. A rectangle is defined by 4 boundaries:

$$h = [a_1 \leq x_1 \leq b_1] \wedge [a_2 \leq x_2 \leq b_2]$$

A car is classified positive if its $(x_1, x_2)$ values fall inside the rectangle, and negative otherwise. This is a very natural hypothesis space — humans often think in terms of "cars with price between $X$ and $Y$, and power between $P$ and $Q$".

Consistent Hypotheses and the Version Space

A hypothesis $h$ is called consistent with the training data if it correctly classifies every single training example — no positive example falls outside $h$, and no negative example falls inside $h$.

The set of all consistent hypotheses is called the version space $VS_{\mathcal{H}, D}$ (where $D$ is the training data):

$$VS_{\mathcal{H},D} = \{ h \in \mathcal{H} \mid h \text{ is consistent with all examples in } D \}$$

As we collect more training examples, the version space shrinks — more hypotheses get eliminated because they misclassify the new examples. Ideally, after enough examples, only the true concept $C$ (or something very close to it) remains.

The S and G Boundaries — The Candidate Elimination Algorithm

For the hypothesis space of axis-parallel rectangles, we can characterize the entire version space with just two hypotheses:

Boundary Name Definition Construction
S Most Specific The smallest rectangle that contains all positive examples Tightest fit — each boundary of S is the extreme-most positive example
G Most General The largest rectangle that excludes all negative examples Largest fit — each boundary of G is just inside the nearest negative example

For rectangles, the most specific hypothesis $S$ is uniquely defined:

$$S = [\min(x_1^+), \max(x_1^+)] \times [\min(x_2^+), \max(x_2^+)]$$

where $x^+$ denotes positive examples. The version space is the set of all rectangles that contain $S$ and are contained in $G$.

⚠ Key Insight
The version space is not empty as long as the training data is consistent (no contradictions — same input point labeled both positive and negative). If the training data is inconsistent, the version space collapses to the empty set.

How Many Examples Are Needed? Sample Complexity

A fundamental question in learning theory is: how many training examples $N$ do we need before the learned hypothesis is close enough to the true concept $C$?

We define error as the probability that a hypothesis $h$ disagrees with the true concept $C$ on a randomly drawn instance:

$$\text{error}(h) = P(h(\mathbf{x}) \neq C(\mathbf{x}))$$

We want a hypothesis where $\text{error}(h) \leq \varepsilon$ for some small $\varepsilon > 0$ (our accuracy parameter). But we want this to hold with high probability (confidence $1 - \delta$ for small $\delta$).

The Bad Hypothesis Problem

A hypothesis $h$ is called $\varepsilon$-bad if $\text{error}(h) > \varepsilon$ — it makes too many errors on new data. Even though an $\varepsilon$-bad hypothesis might be consistent with the training set (by luck), with enough training examples, the probability of this happening becomes small:

$$P(h \text{ consistent with } N \text{ examples}) \leq (1 - \varepsilon)^N$$

Since $(1-\varepsilon)^N \leq e^{-\varepsilon N}$, and applying the union bound over all $|\mathcal{H}|$ hypotheses in the finite hypothesis space:

$$P(\exists \varepsilon\text{-bad } h \text{ consistent with data}) \leq |\mathcal{H}| \cdot e^{-\varepsilon N}$$

We want this probability to be at most $\delta$. Setting $|\mathcal{H}| \cdot e^{-\varepsilon N} \leq \delta$ and solving for $N$:

$$N \geq \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left(\ln |\mathcal{H}| + \ln \frac{1}{\delta}\right)$$
✦ What This Tells Us
  • $N$ scales logarithmically with $|\mathcal{H}|$ — even if the hypothesis space is huge, we don't need exponentially more examples.
  • $N$ scales linearly with $1/\varepsilon$ — to halve the error tolerance, we need twice as many examples.
  • $N$ scales logarithmically with $1/\delta$ — increasing confidence from 95% to 99% costs very little in extra data.

Inductive Bias

Any learning algorithm that generalises beyond the training set must make assumptions — these assumptions are called the inductive bias of the learner. The choice of hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}$ encodes this bias.

🧠 Example of Inductive Bias

If we restrict $\mathcal{H}$ to axis-parallel rectangles, our bias is: "the concept can be described by threshold conditions on individual features independently". This rules out, for example, a concept that is a diagonal strip — which cannot be expressed as a rectangle.

The No Free Lunch Theorem says: there is no universally correct bias. A bias that helps on one problem may hurt on another. The goal is to choose a bias that matches the structure of the real problem.

The Learning Algorithm — Find-S and Candidate Elimination

Two classical algorithms operate on the version space:

Algorithm Strategy Output Drawback
Find-S Start with the most specific hypothesis ($\emptyset$); generalise only when forced by a positive example Most specific consistent hypothesis $S$ Ignores negative examples; may miss the true concept if it's not the most specific
Candidate Elimination Maintain both $S$ (most specific) and $G$ (most general) boundaries; update both with each example Entire version space represented by $S$ and $G$ Expensive for large hypothesis spaces; requires consistent data

Candidate Elimination Update Rules

  1. Positive example $\mathbf{x}^+$ arrives: Generalise $S$ minimally to include $\mathbf{x}^+$. Remove from $G$ any hypothesis that doesn't cover $\mathbf{x}^+$.
  2. Negative example $\mathbf{x}^-$ arrives: Specialise $G$ minimally to exclude $\mathbf{x}^-$. Remove from $S$ any hypothesis that covers $\mathbf{x}^-$.
  3. Check convergence: If $S = G$, the unique consistent hypothesis is found. If $S$ and $G$ cross (version space is empty), the data is inconsistent.

Generalisation and the Role of Bias

Once we have found a consistent hypothesis $h$, how do we classify a new unseen example $\mathbf{x}'$? There are three cases:

Case Condition Decision Confidence
Definite positive $\mathbf{x}'$ is covered by every hypothesis in the version space Classify as positive Certain
Definite negative $\mathbf{x}'$ is rejected by every hypothesis in the version space Classify as negative Certain
Uncertain Some hypotheses in VS cover it, others don't Cannot decide without more data None — more training needed
✦ Practical Takeaway
In practice, when an algorithm has a unique hypothesis (e.g., after training converges), it simply classifies all new inputs using that single hypothesis. The version space framework is a theoretical tool for understanding what the algorithm is doing, and for deriving sample complexity bounds.

Summary of Key Concepts in 2.1

  • Class / Concept $C$: A subset of the input space — the set of all positively-labeled inputs.
  • Hypothesis $h$: A candidate rule that approximates $C$. $h \in \mathcal{H}$ where $\mathcal{H}$ is our assumed hypothesis class.
  • Consistent hypothesis: One that correctly labels every training example.
  • Version space $VS_{\mathcal{H},D}$: The set of all consistent hypotheses. Shrinks with more data.
  • S and G boundaries: Most specific and most general ends of the version space for rectangle hypotheses.
  • Sample complexity: $N \geq \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left(\ln|\mathcal{H}| + \ln\frac{1}{\delta}\right)$ — how much data guarantees learning is accurate with high probability.
  • Inductive bias: The assumptions built into $\mathcal{H}$ that enable generalisation beyond training data.
2.2

Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) Dimension

The sample complexity bound derived in Section 2.1 used $|\mathcal{H}|$ — the number of hypotheses. But for many practical hypothesis spaces (e.g., all linear classifiers in $\mathbb{R}^d$), $|\mathcal{H}| = \infty$. We need a better characterisation of how expressive or complex a hypothesis class is. This is exactly what the VC dimension provides.

Shattering — The Key Concept

We say a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ shatters a set of points $S = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m\}$ if, for every possible labeling of the $m$ points (there are $2^m$ possible labelings), there exists some hypothesis $h \in \mathcal{H}$ that is consistent with that labeling.

📐 Intuition

Shattering means the hypothesis class is rich enough to perfectly separate any conceivable configuration of that set of points. If $\mathcal{H}$ shatters $m$ points, it can "memorise" any possible assignment of $+$/$-$ labels to those $m$ points.

Example: Linear Classifiers in 2D Shattering 3 Points

Consider lines (linear classifiers) in the plane. Can they shatter any 3 points (not all collinear)? Yes — for all $2^3 = 8$ possible labelings of 3 non-collinear points, we can draw a line that separates positives from negatives:

VC Dimension — Formal Definition

✦ Definition: VC Dimension
The VC dimension of a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$, written $\text{VC}(\mathcal{H})$ or $d_{\text{VC}}$, is the cardinality of the largest set of points that $\mathcal{H}$ can shatter. $$\text{VC}(\mathcal{H}) = \max \{ m : \exists S \subseteq \mathcal{X}, |S| = m, \mathcal{H} \text{ shatters } S \}$$ If $\mathcal{H}$ can shatter sets of arbitrarily large size, then $\text{VC}(\mathcal{H}) = \infty$.
⚠ Critical subtlety
We only need to find one set of $m$ points that is shattered — not that every set of $m$ points is shattered. To show $\text{VC}(\mathcal{H}) \geq m$, exhibit one shatterable set of size $m$. To show $\text{VC}(\mathcal{H}) < m$, show that no set of $m$ points can be shattered.

VC Dimension of Common Hypothesis Classes

Hypothesis Class Input Space VC Dimension Why?
Intervals on $\mathbb{R}$: $h_{a,b}(x) = \mathbf{1}[a \leq x \leq b]$ $\mathbb{R}$ 2 Can shatter any 2 points; cannot shatter 3 (if labeled $+−+$, no single interval works)
Linear classifiers (hyperplanes) in $\mathbb{R}^d$: $h_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{x}) = \text{sign}(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + b)$ $\mathbb{R}^d$ $d + 1$ In 1D: lines shatter 2 pts; in 2D: lines shatter 3 pts; in $d$D: hyperplanes shatter $d+1$ pts
Axis-parallel rectangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ $\mathbb{R}^2$ 4 Can shatter 4 points (one at each corner of a rectangle); cannot shatter 5
Convex $k$-gons in $\mathbb{R}^2$ $\mathbb{R}^2$ $\infty$ if $k$ is unbounded Can shatter arbitrarily many points arranged on a circle
Finite set of size $m$ Any $\lfloor \log_2 m \rfloor$ At most $m$ different hypotheses — can represent at most $\log_2 m$ binary bits

VC Dimension of Axis-Parallel Rectangles = 4

For axis-parallel rectangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (the hypothesis from Section 2.1):

  • VC dim ≥ 4: Place 4 points — one at the top, bottom, left, right of a rectangle. For any labeling, we can shrink/expand the rectangle to include/exclude any subset of these 4 points.
  • VC dim < 5: For 5 points in general position, the labeling where the "inner" point is negative and all 4 surrounding points are positive cannot be achieved by any rectangle (a rectangle containing all 4 outer points must also contain the inner point).

The Growth Function and Sauer's Lemma

The growth function $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m)$ counts the maximum number of distinct labelings a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ can produce on any set of $m$ points:

$$\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m) = \max_{S \subseteq \mathcal{X}, |S|=m} |\{ (h(x_1), \ldots, h(x_m)) : h \in \mathcal{H} \}|$$

The growth function is at most $2^m$, with equality precisely when $\mathcal{H}$ shatters some set of size $m$. Sauer's Lemma provides a critical upper bound:

$$\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m) \leq \sum_{i=0}^{d} \binom{m}{i}$$

where $d = \text{VC}(\mathcal{H})$

For $m > d$, this sum is polynomial in $m$ (specifically $O(m^d)$), not exponential. This is the crucial jump from exponential to polynomial complexity!

✦ Why This Matters
  • For $m \leq d$: $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m) = 2^m$ (exponential — very expressive)
  • For $m > d$: $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m) \leq O(m^d)$ (polynomial — quickly saturates)
  • This polynomial bound is what makes learning feasible from finitely many examples

VC Dimension and Sample Complexity

The VC dimension replaces $|\mathcal{H}|$ in the sample complexity bound, enabling a result valid even for infinite hypothesis classes. The VC Generalisation Bound states:

$$P\!\left(\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| > \varepsilon\right) \leq 4 \cdot \Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(2N) \cdot e^{-\varepsilon^2 N / 8}$$

where $R(h)$ is the true (generalisation) error and $\hat{R}(h)$ is the training error. Using Sauer's Lemma to bound $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(2N)$, this gives the practical sample complexity bound:

$$N \geq \frac{8}{\varepsilon^2}\left(d \ln \frac{2eN}{d} + \ln \frac{4}{\delta}\right)$$

This is only slightly worse (by a $\ln N$ factor) than the bound with $|\mathcal{H}|$, but now works for infinite hypothesis classes like all linear classifiers.

VC Dimension for Linear Classifiers — Proof Sketch

Claim: $\text{VC}(\text{hyperplanes in } \mathbb{R}^d) = d + 1$.

  1. Shows VC ≥ d+1: Take the $d+1$ points: $\mathbf{e}_1 = (1,0,\ldots,0)$, $\mathbf{e}_2 = (0,1,\ldots,0)$, …, $\mathbf{e}_d = (0,\ldots,0,1)$, and $\mathbf{0} = (0,\ldots,0)$. For any labeling $y_i \in \{+1,-1\}$, we can find a hyperplane separating positives from negatives. (The Invertibility of the matrix formed by these points guarantees this.)
  2. Shows VC ≤ d+1 (cannot shatter d+2 points): By Radon's theorem, any set of $d+2$ points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ can be split into two groups whose convex hulls intersect. The resulting labeling cannot be linearly separated.
📎 Practical Rule of Thumb
For a linear classifier (perceptron, SVM, logistic regression) with $d$ input features, the VC dimension is exactly $d+1$. This means we need roughly $O(d/\varepsilon^2)$ training examples to achieve error $\varepsilon$. With 100 features, you need on the order of thousands of examples.

Summary of Key Concepts in 2.2

  • Shattering: $\mathcal{H}$ shatters a set $S$ if it can produce every possible binary labeling of $S$.
  • VC dimension $d_{\text{VC}}$: Size of the largest set that $\mathcal{H}$ can shatter — a measure of the effective capacity of the hypothesis class.
  • Key values: Intervals on $\mathbb{R}$: VC = 2; Rectangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$: VC = 4; Hyperplanes in $\mathbb{R}^d$: VC = $d+1$.
  • Sauer's Lemma: $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(m) \leq \sum_{i=0}^{d}\binom{m}{i} = O(m^d)$ — polynomial, not exponential.
  • VC Bound: Generalisation error is bounded using $d_{\text{VC}}$ instead of $|\mathcal{H}|$, enabling results for infinite hypothesis spaces.
  • Bias-Variance tradeoff: Higher VC dim → lower bias (training error) but higher variance (VC penalty). Optimal VC dim balances both.

Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) Learning

PAC learning is a formal mathematical framework for asking: "When can a concept be learned efficiently?". It was introduced by Leslie Valiant in 1984 (Turing Award 2010) and provides the theoretical foundation for understanding learnability.

The key insight: we cannot demand that the learned hypothesis be exactly correct (that may require infinite data). Instead we ask for something approximately correct (error ≤ ε) with high probability (probability ≥ 1 − δ). Hence: Probably Approximately Correct.

Formal PAC Definition

✦ PAC Learnable — Definition

A concept class $\mathcal{C}$ is PAC learnable by hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ using algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ if: for every $\varepsilon \in (0, \frac{1}{2})$, $\delta \in (0, \frac{1}{2})$, every concept $c \in \mathcal{C}$, and every distribution $\mathcal{D}$ over the input space, if $\mathcal{A}$ is given at least $N(\varepsilon, \delta)$ iid samples drawn from $\mathcal{D}$ and labeled by $c$, then $\mathcal{A}$ outputs $h \in \mathcal{H}$ such that:

$$P_{\mathcal{D}}\!\left[\text{error}(h) > \varepsilon\right] \leq \delta$$

and the sample complexity $N(\varepsilon, \delta)$ is polynomial in $1/\varepsilon$, $1/\delta$, and the representation size of the concept.

Breaking Down the Definition

Parameter Meaning Role in PAC
$\varepsilon$ (epsilon) Accuracy / error tolerance We allow up to ε error on new inputs. Smaller ε = stricter requirement.
$\delta$ (delta) Confidence failure probability The algorithm may fail with probability ≤ δ (bad training sample). Smaller δ = higher confidence.
$1 - \delta$ Confidence level With this probability, the algorithm succeeds. Typically 0.95 or 0.99.
$\mathcal{D}$ (distribution) Underlying data distribution The same distribution generates training and test data. PAC holds for all distributions — distribution-free.
Polynomial $N(\varepsilon, \delta)$ Efficient learnability Sample complexity must be polynomial — not exponential — in the relevant parameters. (Exponential would be computationally infeasible.)

PAC Learnability of Axis-Parallel Rectangles

The textbook works through this example in detail. We want to show that the class of axis-parallel rectangles is PAC learnable.

Algorithm: Output the tightest consistent rectangle $R_S$ — the smallest axis-parallel rectangle that contains all positive training examples (the Find-S boundary $S$ from Section 2.1).

The PAC Proof for Rectangles

Let $R^*$ be the true concept (gold rectangle). Divide the boundary region of $R^*$ into 4 strips — one on each side. Define $T_\text{top}$ as the top strip with probability mass exactly $\varepsilon/4$ under $\mathcal{D}$, and similarly $T_\text{bottom}$, $T_\text{left}$, $T_\text{right}$.

The error of the learned $R_S$ exceeds $\varepsilon$ only if $R_S$ misses at least one of the 4 strips entirely. The probability that a strip is missed means no training example fell in that strip, so:

$$P(\text{strip } T_\text{top} \text{ entirely missed by } R_S) \leq \left(1 - \frac{\varepsilon}{4}\right)^N \leq e^{-\varepsilon N / 4}$$

By the union bound over all 4 strips:

$$P(\text{error}(R_S) > \varepsilon) \leq 4 \cdot e^{-\varepsilon N / 4}$$

Setting this ≤ δ and solving for N gives the sample complexity:

$$N \geq \frac{4}{\varepsilon} \ln \frac{4}{\delta}$$
✦ Conclusion
Axis-parallel rectangles are PAC learnable! With $N \geq \frac{4}{\varepsilon}\ln\frac{4}{\delta}$ examples, the tightest consistent rectangle achieves error ≤ ε with probability ≥ 1−δ. The sample complexity is polynomial in $1/\varepsilon$ and $1/\delta$.

Agnostic PAC Learning

Classical PAC learning assumes the target concept $c$ is in the hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ (the realizable case). In practice, the true concept may not be exactly representable — there might be irreducible noise. Agnostic PAC learning relaxes this:

📐 Agnostic PAC Learning

We only require that the algorithm find $h \in \mathcal{H}$ whose error is not much worse than the best possible in $\mathcal{H}$:

$$P\!\left[\text{error}(h) > \min_{h' \in \mathcal{H}} \text{error}(h') + \varepsilon\right] \leq \delta$$

The best hypothesis $h^* = \arg\min_{h\in\mathcal{H}} \text{error}(h)$ has some irreducible error (due to noise or concept not being in $\mathcal{H}$). The goal is to get within $\varepsilon$ of this best achievable error.

Computational PAC Learning

PAC learnability has two components:

  1. Information-theoretic: How many examples are needed? (Sample complexity = polynomial)
  2. Computational: Can the learning algorithm run in polynomial time? (Time complexity = polynomial in $N$, $1/\varepsilon$, $1/\delta$, concept size)

A concept class is efficiently PAC learnable if both sample and time complexity are polynomial. Some classes are information-theoretically PAC learnable but computationally hard (e.g., 3-term DNF formulas are conjectured to be computationally hard to learn, despite having polynomial sample complexity).

⚠ Assumptions in PAC
  • IID samples: Training examples are drawn independently from the same distribution $\mathcal{D}$.
  • Fixed distribution: The same $\mathcal{D}$ governs both training and test inputs.
  • Exact labels (realizable case): Labels come from the true concept $c \in \mathcal{C}$ without noise (relaxed in agnostic PAC).
  • No computational constraints (basic PAC): The algorithm can use infinite time as long as sample count is polynomial.

Connection Between PAC Learning and VC Dimension

The VC dimension characterises PAC learnability in the following fundamental theorem:

✦ Fundamental Theorem of Statistical Learning

A hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ is PAC learnable if and only if its VC dimension is finite.

Moreover, the sample complexity satisfies:

$$C_1 \cdot \frac{d + \ln(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon} \leq N(\varepsilon, \delta) \leq C_2 \cdot \frac{d \ln(1/\varepsilon) + \ln(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon}$$

where $d = \text{VC}(\mathcal{H})$ and $C_1, C_2$ are universal constants.

This is a remarkable result: VC dimension alone determines whether or not a concept class can be learned. It also gives matching upper and lower bounds on sample complexity (up to logarithmic factors).

Summary of Key Concepts in 2.3

  • PAC = Probably Approximately Correct: With probability ≥ 1−δ, the algorithm outputs $h$ with error ≤ ε.
  • ε (accuracy): Allowed error on new data. δ (confidence): Allowable probability of failure.
  • Realizable case: True concept $c \in \mathcal{H}$. Agnostic case: $c \notin \mathcal{H}$, aim for ε-close to best $h^* \in \mathcal{H}$.
  • Rectangles are PAC learnable: $N \geq \frac{4}{\varepsilon}\ln\frac{4}{\delta}$ using the tightest consistent rectangle.
  • Fundamental Theorem: $\mathcal{H}$ is PAC learnable ⟺ $\text{VC}(\mathcal{H}) < \infty$.
  • Efficient PAC: Requires polynomial sample and time complexity. Some classes are info-theoretically learnable but computationally hard.
2.4

Noise

All the analysis so far assumed that the training examples are perfectly labeled by a deterministic true concept $c$. In the real world, however, data is noisy. This section formalises what noise means in a learning context and how it changes our theory.

What Is Noise?

The term noise in machine learning refers to any random, unexplained variability in data. Its sources are:

  • Imprecise measurements: Sensors have limited precision. A temperature sensor may read 37.1°C when the true value is 37.05°C.
  • Non-determinism in the domain: The same input can genuinely produce different outputs — e.g., same symptoms → different diseases in different patients.
  • Missing or unobserved features: Some features relevant to the output are not measured. The unobserved variation looks like noise to the learner.
  • Labeling errors: Human annotators make mistakes; labels may contradict the true class.
⚠ Key Consequence of Noise
Once noise is present, it is impossible to guarantee $\text{error}(h) = 0$. No hypothesis can perfectly fit noisy data. The best we can do is minimise error, and the minimum achievable error (the Bayes error or irreducible error) is strictly greater than zero.

The Probabilistic Model for Noisy Labels

Instead of deterministic labels $y = c(\mathbf{x})$, we model the output as a conditional probability distribution:

$$P(y \mid \mathbf{x}) \quad \text{where } y \in \{0, 1\} \text{ (or } \{+1, -1\})$$

For example, $P(y=1 \mid \mathbf{x}) = 0.8$ means: given input $\mathbf{x}$, there is an 80% chance the label is positive. Two different copies of the same $\mathbf{x}$ might yield different labels. This is the stochastic (noisy) label model.

The Optimal Decision Rule Under Noise

Given $P(y \mid \mathbf{x})$, the lowest-error classifier is the Bayes optimal classifier:

$$h^*(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } P(y=1 \mid \mathbf{x}) \geq 0.5 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

This classifier achieves the Bayes error rate:

$$\varepsilon^* = E_\mathbf{x}\!\left[\min\!\left(P(y=1|\mathbf{x}),\, P(y=0|\mathbf{x})\right)\right]$$

No classifier can do better than $\varepsilon^*$ — it is the fundamental floor set by the noise in the data.

Types of Noise Models

Noise Type Description Effect on Learning
Class Noise (Label Noise) Each example's label is flipped independently with probability $\eta$ (noise rate) Creates inconsistent examples; harder to find a consistent hypothesis. PAC bounds still hold if $\eta < 0.5$.
Attribute Noise The input features $\mathbf{x}$ themselves are measured with random error Corrupts the input representation; different from label noise but also increases error floor.
Class-conditional Noise Noise rate differs per class: positive examples flipped with probability $\eta_+$, negatives with $\eta_-$ More realistic; many denoising and robust learning methods assume this form.

Learning Under Label Noise — The Statistical Setting

With noise rate $\eta$ (symmetric: each label independently flipped with probability $\eta < 0.5$), the noisy label $\tilde{y}$ satisfies:

$$P(\tilde{y} = y) = 1 - \eta, \quad P(\tilde{y} \neq y) = \eta$$

The key insight is that the order of examples by error is preserved: if $\text{error}(h_1) < \text{error}(h_2)$ in the clean setting, the same ordering typically holds in the noisy setting. Therefore, the optimal hypothesis under noiseless conditions is also optimal under symmetric label noise — but it now achieves a higher minimum error.

The effect on PAC sample complexity: the noise inflates the required number of samples only by a constant factor depending on $\eta$:

$$N_{\text{noisy}} \approx \frac{N_{\text{clean}}}{(1 - 2\eta)^2}$$

As $\eta \to 0.5$ (pure noise), the sample complexity blows up — the problem becomes exponentially harder. At $\eta = 0.5$, learning is impossible (labels are pure random).

Overfitting Under Noise

A critical issue: if a model is very complex (high VC dimension), it can memorise the noisy labels in the training set, achieving near-zero training error. But this comes at the cost of very high test error because it has learned the noise, not the signal. This is called overfitting.

🧠 Noise vs Complexity
  • Low-complexity model: may not fit the signal well (underfitting), but is not fooled by noise
  • High-complexity model: perfectly fits the training data (including label noise), but generalises poorly (overfitting)
  • Right complexity: matches the signal, ignores the noise → best generalisation

This is the key reason why model selection (regularisation, cross-validation) is essential in the presence of noise.

Noise in the PAC Framework

The PAC model can handle noise through the Statistical Query (SQ) model (Kearns 1993). Instead of direct labeled examples, the learner can query statistics of the form $E[f(\mathbf{x}, y)]$ with small error $\tau$. Intuitively, averaging over many examples cancels out random label flips.

Any PAC algorithm that works via statistical queries (no individual example inspection) is automatically robust to classification noise with rate $\eta < \frac{\tau}{2}$. Most classical ML algorithms (Naive Bayes, neural networks, k-NN) are SQ algorithms.

Summary — Section 2.4

  • Noise sources: Imprecise measurements, non-determinism, missing features, labeling errors.
  • Stochastic model: Output $y \sim P(y|\mathbf{x})$ instead of $y = c(\mathbf{x})$.
  • Bayes error $\varepsilon^*$: Irreducible minimum error = $E_\mathbf{x}[\min(P(y=1|\mathbf{x}), P(y=0|\mathbf{x}))]$.
  • Label noise rate $\eta$: Symmetric flip probability; inflates sample complexity by $1/(1-2\eta)^2$.
  • Overfitting: Complex models memorise noise; need regularisation or simpler models.
  • SQ model: Statistical query model makes PAC learning robust to noise.
2.5

Learning Multiple Classes

Sections 2.1–2.4 focused on binary classification: positive vs negative (two classes). Most real problems involve more than two classes. This section extends the framework to multiclass classification.

Problem Setup — K Classes

Instead of class $C$ vs $\overline{C}$, we now have $K$ classes $C_1, C_2, \ldots, C_K$. The class label $y \in \{1, 2, \ldots, K\}$. Each input $\mathbf{x}$ belongs to exactly one class.

📐 Multiclass Discriminant

Instead of one hypothesis $h: \mathcal{X} \to \{0,1\}$, we learn $K$ discriminant functions $g_k(\mathbf{x})$, one per class:

$$\hat{y} = \arg\max_{k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}} g_k(\mathbf{x})$$

The predicted class is the one with the highest discriminant value. For probabilistic models, $g_k(\mathbf{x}) = P(C_k \mid \mathbf{x})$.

Reduction to Binary: One-vs-Rest and One-vs-One

Many binary classifiers can be extended to multiple classes using two standard reduction strategies:

Strategy Construction #Classifiers Drawback
One-vs-Rest (OvR) For each class $k$, train a binary classifier: $C_k$ vs all others. Predict $\arg\max_k g_k(\mathbf{x})$. $K$ Class imbalance (one class vs. $K-1$ others); ambiguous regions where multiple $g_k > 0$.
One-vs-One (OvO) Train a binary classifier for every pair $(C_i, C_j)$. Predict by majority vote. $K(K-1)/2$ Quadratic growth in number of classifiers; each uses only 2-class data (wastes information).

Multiclass PAC Learning

The PAC framework extends naturally: we want $P[\text{error}(h) > \varepsilon] \leq \delta$ where the error is now a multiclass error (probability of predicting the wrong class label). The sample complexity bound becomes:

$$N \geq \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left(\ln|\mathcal{H}| + \ln\frac{K}{\delta}\right)$$

The extra $\ln K$ factor (over binary PAC) comes from applying the union bound over $K$ classes. With a large number of classes, we need slightly more data — but only logarithmically more.

Multiclass Version Space

For multiclass problems with hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ of discriminant functions, the version space still tracks all hypotheses consistent with the training data. The key difference: now an error occurs whenever $\hat{y} \neq y$ for any of the $K$ classes, not just positive/negative. The VC dimension generalises via the Natarajan dimension for multiclass settings.

✦ Practical tip: Softmax outputs
For probabilistic classifiers (logistic regression, neural networks) with $K$ classes, the outputs are modelled as a probability distribution over classes using the softmax function: $$P(C_k \mid \mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{g_k(\mathbf{x})}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{g_j(\mathbf{x})}}$$ This guarantees outputs are non-negative and sum to 1, forming a valid probability distribution. The predicted class is still $\arg\max_k P(C_k | \mathbf{x})$.

Summary — Section 2.5

  • Multiclass setup: $K$ classes $C_1,\ldots,C_K$; learn $K$ discriminant functions $g_k(\mathbf{x})$, predict $\arg\max_k g_k(\mathbf{x})$.
  • OvR: $K$ binary classifiers (one per class vs all). OvO: $K(K-1)/2$ binary classifiers (one per pair).
  • Sample complexity: $\frac{1}{\varepsilon}(\ln|\mathcal{H}| + \ln\frac{K}{\delta})$ — only $\ln K$ more than binary.
  • Softmax: Converts $K$ unbounded discriminant scores to a valid probability distribution.
2.6

Regression

In classification, the output $y$ is a discrete class label. In regression, $y \in \mathbb{R}$ is a real-valued continuous output. Everything from the PAC framework and version space carries over, with the loss function changed from 0/1 loss to a continuous loss.

The Regression Setup

We observe training data $\{(\mathbf{x}^t, r^t)\}_{t=1}^N$ where $r^t \in \mathbb{R}$ is the real-valued response. We assume:

$$r = g(\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon \quad \text{where } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

$g(\mathbf{x})$ is the true regression function (unknown), and $\epsilon$ is zero-mean Gaussian noise with variance $\sigma^2$. Our goal: find $\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})$ that minimises prediction error on new inputs.

✦ Loss Function for Regression
The standard loss is the squared error: $$L(r, \hat{g}(\mathbf{x})) = (r - \hat{g}(\mathbf{x}))^2$$ The expected loss (risk) is: $$R(\hat{g}) = E_{\mathbf{x},r}\!\left[(r - \hat{g}(\mathbf{x}))^2\right]$$ The empirical (training) risk is: $$\hat{R}(\hat{g}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N} (r^t - \hat{g}(\mathbf{x}^t))^2 \equiv \text{MSE (Mean Squared Error)}$$

Polynomial Regression — The Core Example

The most common parametric regression model is a polynomial. For a single input $x$:

$$\hat{g}(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} w_j x^j = w_0 + w_1 x + w_2 x^2 + \cdots + w_k x^k$$

where $k$ is the polynomial degree (complexity) and $\mathbf{w} = (w_0, w_1, \ldots, w_k)$ are the parameters to be learned. Although the model is a polynomial in $x$, it is linear in the parameters $\mathbf{w}$ — this makes optimisation tractable via the ordinary least squares (OLS) formula.

Ordinary Least Squares Solution

Define the design matrix $\mathbf{\Phi} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times (k+1)}$ where $\Phi_{tj} = (x^t)^j$, and the output vector $\mathbf{r} = (r^1, \ldots, r^N)^T$. The OLS solution is:

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi})^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{r}$$

This is the closed-form solution — no iterative optimisation needed! It minimises the sum of squared residuals exactly. The matrix $(\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi})^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T$ is called the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse.

The Regression PAC Bound

For regression, the ε-error condition is analogous: we want the expected squared error on new data to be at most ε. The key difference from classification is that we use squared loss instead of 0/1 loss. A hypothesis $h$ is called $\varepsilon$-accurate if:

$$E\!\left[(r - h(\mathbf{x}))^2\right] \leq \varepsilon$$

The sample complexity bound for regression with a polynomial hypothesis space of degree $k$ is:

$$N \geq \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left((k+1)\ln\frac{1}{\varepsilon} + \ln\frac{1}{\delta}\right)$$

The VC dimension of $k$-degree polynomials (in 1D) is $k+1$, so this is consistent with the general VC bound. In $d$ input dimensions, the sample complexity scales with the number of parameters $\binom{d+k}{k}$.

Summary — Section 2.6

  • Regression: Output $r \in \mathbb{R}$; model $r = g(\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon$ with Gaussian noise.
  • Loss function: Squared error $(r - \hat{g}(\mathbf{x}))^2$; empirical risk = MSE.
  • Polynomial regression: $\hat{g}(x) = \sum_{j=0}^k w_j x^j$; solved exactly by OLS $\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{\Phi})^{-1}\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$.
  • Degree $k$: VC dimension = $k+1$; higher $k$ → lower bias, higher variance — eventually overfits.
2.7

Model Selection and Generalisation

We have established that hypothesis complexity (VC dimension) determines a tradeoff between training error and generalisation. How do we choose the right complexity in practice? This is the model selection problem.

The Generalisation Gap

The generalisation gap is the difference between the true error and the training error:

$$\text{generalisation gap} = R(h) - \hat{R}(h)$$

The VC theory tells us this gap is bounded by a function of VC dimension and sample size:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \Omega\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{d_{\text{VC}}\ln(N/d_{\text{VC}}) + \ln(1/\delta)}{N}}\right)$$

where the second term is the complexity penalty $\Omega$. The total true error = training error + complexity penalty. Model selection means finding the model complexity that minimises this total.

Bias-Variance Decomposition

For a regression estimator $\hat{g}$, the expected squared error at a point $\mathbf{x}$ decomposes exactly as:

$$E\!\left[(r - \hat{g}(\mathbf{x}))^2\right] = \underbrace{\left(\text{Bias}[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})]\right)^2}_{\text{systematic error}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})]}_{\text{random error}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible noise}}$$

where:

  • $\text{Bias}[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})] = E[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})] - g(\mathbf{x})$ — the systematic gap between the average prediction and the truth. Caused by the model being too simple.
  • $\text{Var}[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})] = E[(\hat{g}(\mathbf{x}) - E[\hat{g}(\mathbf{x})])^2]$ — how much the prediction varies across different training sets. Caused by the model being too sensitive to fluctuations in training data.
  • $\sigma^2$ — irreducible noise; cannot be reduced regardless of model choice.

Model Selection Strategies

1. Structural Risk Minimisation (SRM)

Organise hypothesis classes from simplest to most complex: $\mathcal{H}_1 \subset \mathcal{H}_2 \subset \ldots$ with increasing VC dimension $d_1 < d_2 < \ldots$ Choose the class $\mathcal{H}_k$ that minimises the bound:

$$k^* = \arg\min_{k} \left[\hat{R}(h_k) + \Omega_k(N, \delta)\right]$$

where $\Omega_k$ is the complexity penalty for VC dimension $d_k$. This is a principled, theory-driven approach but requires computing VC dimensions.

2. Validation / Hold-Out

Hold out a validation set (typically 20–30% of data). Train each candidate model on the training set, evaluate its error on the validation set. Choose the model with lowest validation error. This is the most practical and widely used approach.

3. Cross-Validation (CV)

Divide data into $K$ folds. For each candidate model complexity, train on $K-1$ folds and evaluate on the remaining fold — repeat $K$ times, average the error. The complexity with lowest CV error is selected. More data-efficient than a single hold-out split.

✦ The Complete Workflow
  1. Split data: train / validation / test (60/20/20 typical)
  2. For each model complexity (e.g., $k = 1,2,\ldots,K$): train on training set, evaluate validation error
  3. Select complexity $k^*$ with lowest validation error
  4. Retrain final model on train+validation with complexity $k^*$
  5. Report final error on test set (used only once)

Occam's Razor in ML

The famous philosophical principle — Occam's Razor — says: "Among all hypotheses that fit the data, prefer the simplest one." In ML, this is formalised as: if two hypotheses achieve the same training error, prefer the one with smaller VC dimension (less complex), because the simpler one has lower complexity penalty $\Omega$, and therefore a stronger guarantee on generalisation.

🧠 Occam's Razor ≠ Always Right
Occam's Razor is a useful heuristic but not universally correct. Modern deep learning uses extremely over-parameterised models (billions of parameters) that still generalise well — a phenomenon not yet fully explained by classical VC theory. This is an active research area.

Summary — Section 2.7

  • Generalisation gap: $R(h) - \hat{R}(h)$; bounded by complexity penalty $\Omega(d_{\text{VC}}, N)$.
  • Total error = Bias² + Variance + σ²: Increasing complexity trades bias for variance.
  • Model selection strategies: SRM (theory), validation set (practical), cross-validation (data-efficient).
  • Occam's Razor: Given equal training error, prefer simpler model (smaller VC dim).

Parametric Classification

In parametric classification we assume that class-conditional densities belong to a known parametric family (e.g., Gaussian), estimate the parameters from training data, and then classify new points using Bayes' theorem. This section is the bridge between density estimation and decision-making.

Setup and Notation

We have $K$ classes $C_1, \ldots, C_K$ with prior probabilities $P(C_k)$. For each class we model the class-conditional density $p(\mathbf{x} | C_k)$. Bayes' theorem gives the posterior:

$$P(C_k | \mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x} | C_k)\, P(C_k)}{p(\mathbf{x})} \qquad \text{where} \quad p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^K p(\mathbf{x} | C_j)\, P(C_j)$$

The Bayes optimal classifier assigns $\mathbf{x}$ to the class with the highest posterior:

$$\hat{k} = \arg\max_{k} P(C_k | \mathbf{x}) = \arg\max_{k}\; p(\mathbf{x} | C_k)\, P(C_k)$$
✦ Why Parametric?
Non-parametric methods (KDE, k-NN) estimate density from scratch — expensive and require lots of data in high dimensions. The parametric approach restricts to a family (e.g., Gaussian), dramatically reducing what must be estimated, at the cost of a modelling assumption.

Univariate Gaussian Case (d = 1)

Assume each class has a Gaussian (Normal) class-conditional density:

$$p(x | C_k) = \mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu_k, \sigma_k^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_k^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \mu_k)^2}{2\sigma_k^2}\right)$$

Given training samples $\{x_i^{(k)}\}_{i=1}^{N_k}$ for class $k$, the maximum likelihood estimates are:

$$\hat{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_{i=1}^{N_k} x_i^{(k)}, \qquad \hat{\sigma}_k^2 = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_{i=1}^{N_k}(x_i^{(k)} - \hat{\mu}_k)^2$$

The Decision Boundary

For two classes, we classify $x$ as $C_1$ if $p(x|C_1)P(C_1) > p(x|C_2)P(C_2)$, i.e., if the log-likelihood ratio exceeds a threshold:

$$\ln \frac{p(x|C_1)}{p(x|C_2)} > \ln \frac{P(C_2)}{P(C_1)}$$

Case 1: Equal Variances $\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2 = \sigma^2$ → Linear Boundary

Expanding the log-likelihood ratio with equal variances, the $x^2$ terms cancel and we get a linear discriminant:

$$\frac{\mu_1 - \mu_2}{\sigma^2} x - \frac{\mu_1^2 - \mu_2^2}{2\sigma^2} > \ln\frac{P(C_2)}{P(C_1)}$$

This gives a single threshold $x_0$ (a point on the number line) that separates the two classes. The boundary is linear in $x$.

Case 2: Unequal Variances → Quadratic Boundary

When $\sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2$, the $x^2$ terms do not cancel and we get a quadratic discriminant — the boundary is a degree-2 polynomial in $x$ (potentially two thresholds).

The Multivariate Gaussian Classifier

Extending to $d$ dimensions, each class has a multivariate Gaussian class-conditional:

$$p(\mathbf{x} | C_k) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)\right)$$

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA): Shared Covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}$

When all classes share the same covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, the quadratic terms in $\mathbf{x}^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{x}$ cancel and the log-posterior becomes linear in $\mathbf{x}$:

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{x} - \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k + \ln P(C_k)$$

Classify $\mathbf{x}$ to class $\hat{k} = \arg\max_k g_k(\mathbf{x})$. The decision boundary between $C_i$ and $C_j$ is the hyperplane $g_i(\mathbf{x}) = g_j(\mathbf{x})$.

Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA): Class-Specific Covariances

When $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$ differ, the decision rule retains the quadratic term:

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = -\frac{1}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k| - \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k) + \ln P(C_k)$$

The boundary between two classes is a quadric surface (ellipsoid, paraboloid, hyperboloid, etc.). QDA is more flexible but requires estimating $K \cdot d(d+1)/2$ covariance parameters vs. just $d(d+1)/2$ for LDA.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation for Parametric Classifiers

For class $k$ with $N_k$ training samples $\{\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}\}$, the ML estimates are:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_{i=1}^{N_k}\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}, \qquad \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_k = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_{i=1}^{N_k}(\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)(\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)^T, \qquad \hat{P}(C_k) = \frac{N_k}{N}$$

For LDA, the shared covariance is the pooled within-class covariance:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\sum_{i=1}^{N_k}(\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)(\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)}-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)^T = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K} N_k \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_k$$

Naïve Bayes Classifier

The Naïve Bayes assumption is that features are conditionally independent given the class:

$$p(\mathbf{x}|C_k) = \prod_{j=1}^d p(x_j | C_k)$$

This is "naïve" because real features are almost never independent. Yet Naïve Bayes often works remarkably well in practice (especially in text classification), because even if the probabilities are wrong, the argmax of the posterior is often correct.

  • Naïve Bayes: Conditional independence assumption; simple but surprisingly effective.
4.6

Regression

Regression models a continuous output $r \in \mathbb{R}$ given input $\mathbf{x}$. In the parametric approach we assume the output is generated as a function of $\mathbf{x}$ plus noise:

$$r = g(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{w}) + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

where $g(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{w})$ is the regression function parameterised by $\mathbf{w}$, and $\varepsilon$ is zero-mean Gaussian noise. The goal is to find $\mathbf{w}$ that minimises prediction error.

Linear Regression

The simplest case: $g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + w_0$. We absorb the bias by prepending 1 to $\mathbf{x}$, so $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$. For $N$ training pairs $\{(\mathbf{x}_i, r_i)\}$:

$$\text{Minimise} \quad E(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^N (r_i - \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i)^2 = \|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{\Phi}\mathbf{w}\|^2$$

where $\mathbf{\Phi}$ is the $N \times (d+1)$ design matrix with $\mathbf{\Phi}_{ij} = x_{ij}$. Setting $\nabla_\mathbf{w} E = 0$:

$$\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{\Phi}\, \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{r} \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{OLS}} = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{\Phi})^{-1}\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$$

This is the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) solution, also called the normal equations or the pseudoinverse formula: $\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{\Phi}^+\mathbf{r}$.

✦ MLE Connection
Minimising the sum of squared errors is equivalent to maximum likelihood estimation under a Gaussian noise assumption. The Gaussian likelihood is $p(\mathbf{r}|\mathbf{w}) = \prod_i \mathcal{N}(r_i \mid \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i, \sigma^2)$. Maximising this is the same as minimising $\sum_i (r_i - \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i)^2$.

Polynomial Regression

Linear regression in $\mathbf{x}$ is too restrictive for nonlinear relationships. We map $\mathbf{x}$ into a higher-dimensional feature space using basis functions $\phi_j(x)$:

$$g(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} w_j\, \phi_j(x), \qquad \text{e.g.}\; \phi_j(x) = x^j$$

The model is still linear in the parameters $\mathbf{w}$, so the same OLS formula applies with the new design matrix $\mathbf{\Phi}_{ij} = \phi_j(x_i)$. The degree $k$ is a hyperparameter controlling model complexity.

Residuals and Model Assumptions

After fitting, the residuals are $e_i = r_i - \hat{r}_i$. Under a correctly specified model, residuals should be:

  • Approximately Normally distributed (Q-Q plot check)
  • Homoscedastic — constant variance across predicted values
  • Independent — no autocorrelation structure

Departures from these assumptions indicate model misspecification, requiring a different basis or transformation of $\mathbf{x}$.

Summary — Section 4.6

  • OLS formula: $\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{\Phi})^{-1}\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$ — closed-form, unique solution when $\mathbf{\Phi}^T\mathbf{\Phi}$ is invertible.
  • Equivalent to MLE under Gaussian noise assumption.
  • Polynomial regression = linear model in a feature space $\phi_j(x) = x^j$.
  • Degree $k$ is a hyperparameter controlling bias-variance tradeoff.

Tuning Model Complexity: Bias/Variance Dilemma

The bias-variance dilemma is one of the most fundamental concepts in machine learning. It explains the tradeoff between a model's ability to fit the training data (low bias) and its sensitivity to fluctuations in the training set (low variance).

The Bias-Variance Decomposition

For a regression problem with target $r = f(\mathbf{x}) + \varepsilon$ where $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon]=0$, $\text{Var}(\varepsilon)=\sigma^2$, the expected mean squared error of our learner $\hat{f}$ at a point $\mathbf{x}$ is:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[(r - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}))^2\right] = \underbrace{\left(\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})] - f(\mathbf{x})\right)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})]\right)^2\right]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Noise}}$$

Noise σ² Irreducible error Inherent randomness in the data generation Cannot be reduced (irreducible)
⚠ The Fundamental Tension
Reducing bias usually increases variance, and vice versa. A simpler model (e.g., linear) has high bias (it can't represent complex patterns) but low variance (insensitive to particular training set). A complex model (e.g., high-degree polynomial) has low bias but high variance (fits noise along with signal).

Bias-Variance in Classification

For classification with 0/1 loss, the decomposition is more complex, but the intuition holds:

  • A model with high bias consistently misclassifies the same instances — it has a systematic flaw.
  • A model with high variance is inconsistent — trained on slightly different data it makes very different predictions.

How Complexity Affects Each Term

Model Bias Variance Training Error Test Error
Linear (k=1) High Low High High (underfitting)
Polynomial k=2–3 Medium Medium Low-medium Low (good fit)
High-degree k≫ Very Low Very High ≈ 0 High (overfitting)

Summary — Section 4.7

  • Decomposition: $\text{MSE} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \sigma^2$.
  • Bias: Systematic error from wrong model assumptions. Reduced by more complexity.
  • Variance: Sensitivity to training set. Reduced by regularisation or more data.
  • Noise $\sigma^2$: Irreducible — cannot be reduced by any model change.
  • Goal: choose model complexity to minimise $\text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance}$ (the controllable terms).
4.8

Model Selection Procedures

Model selection is the process of choosing among competing models (different hypothesis classes, or the same class with different hyperparameters) based on their expected generalisation performance. We cannot use training error — a more complex model always fits training data better. We need an estimate of test error.

The Three-Set Split

The gold standard is to divide data into three non-overlapping sets:

Training set → fit model parameters (w)
Validation set → select among models / tune hyperparameters
Test set → report final unbiased error estimate (used ONCE at the very end)
⚠ Do Not Peek at the Test Set
Once a model is selected using the validation set, retrain on training+validation, then evaluate on test. If you pick the model that does best on test, you've effectively used test as a second validation set and the reported error will be optimistic.

Cross-Validation for Model Selection

When data is limited, a single validation split wastes data. K-fold cross-validation reuses all data systematically:

  1. Split data into $K$ equal folds $F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_K$.
  2. For model candidate $m$: for each fold $i$, train on all folds except $F_i$, evaluate on $F_i$. Record error $e_i^{(m)}$.
  3. CV estimate for model $m$: $\text{CV}(m) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^K e_i^{(m)}$.
  4. Select model $m^* = \arg\min_m \text{CV}(m)$.
  5. Retrain $m^*$ on all data. Evaluate on the held-out test set.

Information Criteria: AIC and BIC

Instead of cross-validation, information criteria provide analytical estimates of generalisation error that include a penalty for model complexity. They can be computed from a single fit (no resampling needed).

$$\text{AIC} = -2\ln \hat{L} + 2k \qquad \text{(Akaike Information Criterion)}$$ $$\text{BIC} = -2\ln \hat{L} + k\ln N \qquad \text{(Bayesian Information Criterion)}$$

where $\hat{L}$ is the maximised log-likelihood, $k$ is the number of free parameters, and $N$ is the number of training examples. Lower AIC/BIC = better model.

Criterion Penalty per parameter Tendency When to prefer
AIC $2$ Selects larger models When the true model is complex; or when N is not large
BIC $\ln N$ Selects smaller models (for $N \geq 8$ since $\ln 8 > 2$) When you believe a simpler model is adequate; large N

Regularisation as Implicit Model Selection

Rather than choosing among discrete model families, regularisation penalises model complexity continuously. For regression:

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \arg\min_\mathbf{w} \left[\sum_{i=1}^N (r_i - \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i)^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{w}\|^2\right]$$

The parameter $\lambda \geq 0$ controls the strength of regularisation. As $\lambda$ increases, the model is forced to use smaller weights → simpler model → higher bias but lower variance. This gives a smooth continuum of model complexities parameterised by $\lambda$ (a hyperparameter selected by cross-validation).

Summary — Section 4.8

  • Model selection uses validation/CV error, never training error.
  • Three-set split: Training → fit parameters; Validation → select model; Test → report final error (use once).
  • K-fold CV: $\text{CV}(m) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_i e_i^{(m)}$. Select $m^* = \arg\min_m \text{CV}(m)$.
  • AIC: $-2\ln\hat{L} + 2k$. BIC: $-2\ln\hat{L} + k\ln N$ (stronger penalty; prefers simpler models for large $N$).
  • Regularisation: Continuous model selection via $\lambda$ — larger $\lambda$ = simpler model. Choose $\lambda$ by CV.
10.6

Gradient Descent

Most machine learning objectives do not have a closed-form minimiser. Gradient descent is the workhorse iterative algorithm that minimises a differentiable loss function $E(\mathbf{w})$ by repeatedly stepping in the direction of steepest descent (negative gradient).

$$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta\, \nabla_\mathbf{w} E(\mathbf{w}^{(t)})$$

Here $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate (step size). Each iteration moves the parameter vector a small step against the gradient — like a ball rolling downhill on an error surface.

✦ Geometric Intuition
The gradient $\nabla E(\mathbf{w})$ points in the direction of steepest increase of $E$. Subtracting it from $\mathbf{w}$ moves us toward lower error. The learning rate $\eta$ controls how large each step is.

The Error Surface

Think of $E(\mathbf{w})$ as a landscape in weight space. For squared error with a linear model, the surface is a bowl (paraboloid) with a unique global minimum. For neural networks, it is a complicated non-convex landscape with saddle points, local minima, and flat plateaus.

Learning Rate: The Critical Hyperparameter

The learning rate $\eta$ is the most important hyperparameter of gradient descent:

$\eta$ valueBehaviourDiagnosis
Too largeError increases or oscillates/divergesOvershoots the minimum every step
Too smallVery slow convergence, many iterationsTiny steps; wastes computation
Just rightSmooth, fast convergenceTypically found by grid search or schedules

Three Flavours of Gradient Descent

How much data is used to estimate the gradient determines the variant:

1. Batch Gradient Descent (BGD)

Compute the gradient using all $N$ training examples at once:

$$\nabla E(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \nabla E_i(\mathbf{w})$$

Pro: Exact (low-variance) gradient. Smooth convergence on convex problems.
Con: Very slow for large $N$ — each update requires a full pass through data.

2. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

Update parameters after each single example $(i$ chosen randomly):

$$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta\, \nabla E_i(\mathbf{w}^{(t)})$$

Pro: Very fast updates. The noise can help escape local minima and saddle points.
Con: Noisy gradient estimates → erratic path; may not converge but oscillates near minimum.

3. Mini-Batch Gradient Descent

Update using a random mini-batch of $B$ examples ($B$ typically 32–256):

$$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta\, \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_t} \nabla E_i(\mathbf{w}^{(t)})$$

Pro: Best of both worlds — reduced variance vs. SGD; faster than BGD; hardware-efficient (vectorised). The standard in deep learning.
Con: Introduces batch size as an additional hyperparameter.

Convergence Conditions

For a convex function with Lipschitz-continuous gradients and step size $\eta \leq \frac{1}{L}$ (where $L$ is the Lipschitz constant), batch gradient descent converges with error $O(1/t)$ after $t$ iterations. SGD achieves $O(1/\sqrt{t})$.

📐 Practical Stopping Criteria
  • Fixed epochs: Stop after a predetermined number of passes over the data.
  • Gradient norm: Stop when $\|\nabla E(\mathbf{w})\| < \epsilon$ (gradient is small → near stationary point).
  • Loss change: Stop when $|E^{(t)} - E^{(t-1)}| < \epsilon$ (improvement plateaued).
  • Validation error: Stop when validation error stops improving (early stopping).

Learning Rate Schedules and Adaptive Methods

A fixed $\eta$ is rarely optimal throughout training. Common strategies:

  • Step decay: Reduce $\eta$ by a factor every $k$ epochs. E.g., $\eta_t = \eta_0 \cdot 0.1^{\lfloor t/k \rfloor}$.
  • Cosine annealing: $\eta_t = \eta_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min})(1 + \cos(\pi t/T))$.
  • Momentum: Accumulate a velocity vector: $\mathbf{v}^{(t+1)} = \gamma \mathbf{v}^{(t)} - \eta\nabla E$; $\;\mathbf{w} \mathrel{+}= \mathbf{v}^{(t+1)}$. Accelerates in consistent gradient directions.
  • Adam: Adaptive per-parameter learning rates using first and second moment estimates of the gradient — the most widely used optimiser in deep learning.

Summary — Section 10.6

  • Update rule: $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\,\nabla E(\mathbf{w})$.
  • Three variants: Batch (exact gradient, slow), SGD (one sample, noisy), Mini-batch (practical standard).
  • Learning rate $\eta$ is the key hyperparameter — too large: diverges; too small: very slow.
  • On convex surfaces: guaranteed convergence. On non-convex: may hit local minima/saddle points.
  • Advanced: momentum, Adam, learning rate schedules improve convergence in practice.
10.7

Logistic Discrimination

Logistic discrimination (logistic regression) is a discriminative classifier: instead of modelling the class-conditional densities $p(\mathbf{x}|C_k)$ and applying Bayes' rule (generative approach), it directly models the posterior $P(C_k|\mathbf{x})$ as a parametric function of $\mathbf{x}$. This avoids the need to model the input distribution and often gives better classification performance.

📐 Generative vs Discriminative
ApproachWhat is modelled?Example
Generative$p(\mathbf{x}|C_k)$ and $P(C_k)$, then use BayesGaussian (LDA/QDA), Naïve Bayes
Discriminative$P(C_k|\mathbf{x})$ directlyLogistic regression, SVMs

10.7.1 — Two-Class Logistic Discrimination

For two classes $C_1$ (label $r=1$) and $C_2$ (label $r=0$), the model is:

$$P(C_1 | \mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + w_0) = \sigma(a), \qquad \text{where}\quad \sigma(a) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-a}}$$

$\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid (logistic) function. It maps any real number to $(0,1)$, making it a valid probability. The complement gives $P(C_2|\mathbf{x}) = 1 - \sigma(a) = \sigma(-a)$.

The linear combination $a = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + w_0$ is called the log-odds or logit:

$$a = \ln \frac{P(C_1|\mathbf{x})}{P(C_2|\mathbf{x})} = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + w_0$$

The decision boundary is where $P(C_1|\mathbf{x}) = 0.5$, i.e., $a = 0$, which is the hyperplane $\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + w_0 = 0$ — a linear boundary in input space.

Maximum Likelihood Training (Cross-Entropy Loss)

Given training data $\{(\mathbf{x}_i, r_i)\}$ with $r_i \in \{0,1\}$, the log-likelihood under the Bernoulli model is:

$$\ln \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[r_i \ln y_i + (1-r_i)\ln(1-y_i)\right], \qquad y_i = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i)$$

We minimise the negative log-likelihood (the cross-entropy loss):

$$E(\mathbf{w}) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[r_i \ln y_i + (1-r_i)\ln(1-y_i)\right]$$

Unlike linear regression, there is no closed-form solution. The gradient is:

$$\nabla_\mathbf{w} E = \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - r_i)\, \mathbf{x}_i$$
✦ Beautiful Gradient Formula
The gradient $(y_i - r_i)\mathbf{x}_i$ has the same form as linear regression! The error signal $(y_i - r_i)$ — predicted probability minus true label — weights each input $\mathbf{x}_i$. This makes the gradient descent update rule clean and interpretable.

The weight update at each step of gradient descent is:

$$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta \sum_{i=1}^{N}(y_i^{(t)} - r_i)\,\mathbf{x}_i$$

The cross-entropy loss for logistic regression is convex in $\mathbf{w}$, so gradient descent is guaranteed to find the global minimum.

Regularised Logistic Regression

Adding $L_2$ regularisation prevents overfitting (especially important when features outnumber examples):

$$E_{\text{reg}}(\mathbf{w}) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[r_i\ln y_i + (1-r_i)\ln(1-y_i)\right] + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$$
$$\nabla E_{\text{reg}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N}(y_i - r_i)\,\mathbf{x}_i + \lambda\mathbf{w}$$

10.7.2 — Multi-Class Logistic Discrimination

For $K > 2$ mutually exclusive classes, the two-class sigmoid generalises to the softmax function:

$$P(C_k | \mathbf{x}) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{w}_k^T \mathbf{x})}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \exp(\mathbf{w}_j^T \mathbf{x})} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{a})_k, \qquad a_k = \mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}$$

Each class $k$ has its own weight vector $\mathbf{w}_k$. The softmax ensures all class probabilities are positive and sum to 1: $\sum_k P(C_k|\mathbf{x}) = 1$.

🔮 Softmax Properties
  • Invariant to adding a constant to all logits: $\text{softmax}(\mathbf{a} + c) = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{a})$ → in practice subtract $\max_k a_k$ for numerical stability.
  • When one logit is much larger than all others, the output ≈ 1 for that class and ≈ 0 for all others (sharp distribution).
  • Reduces to sigmoid for $K=2$.

Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss

Let $r_{ik} = 1$ if example $i$ belongs to class $k$, else 0 (one-hot encoding). The loss is:

$$E(\{\mathbf{w}_k\}) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K} r_{ik} \ln y_{ik}, \qquad y_{ik} = P(C_k|\mathbf{x}_i)$$

The gradient with respect to $\mathbf{w}_k$ is:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{w}_k} E = \sum_{i=1}^{N}(y_{ik} - r_{ik})\,\mathbf{x}_i$$

Same form as the two-class case! The error for each class $(y_{ik} - r_{ik})$ weights the input. All $K$ weight vectors are updated simultaneously.

Interpreting Softmax Outputs

The output $y_k = P(C_k|\mathbf{x})$ gives a calibrated probability estimate for each class. For prediction we take $\hat{k} = \arg\max_k y_k$. The entire output vector is useful when we need uncertainty estimates (e.g., rejecting low-confidence predictions).

10.7.3 — Multi-Label Classification

In multi-label classification each instance can simultaneously belong to any subset of $K$ classes. This is fundamentally different from multi-class:

SettingClasses per instanceClass probabilitiesOutput activation
Binary (K=2)Exactly 1Sum = 1Sigmoid (1 output)
Multi-class (K>2)Exactly 1Sum = 1Softmax (K outputs)
Multi-label0 to K simultaneouslyEach ∈ (0,1) independentlyK independent sigmoids

Multi-label with Independent Sigmoids

The standard approach: train $K$ independent binary logistic classifiers, one per class. For each class $k$:

$$P(y_k=1 | \mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}), \qquad k = 1, \ldots, K$$

Each sigmoid output is a score for the $k$-th label. We predict label $k$ as present if $\sigma(\mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}) > 0.5$ (or a tuned threshold). The outputs do not need to sum to 1.

Multi-label Loss: Binary Cross-Entropy Per Label

Let $r_{ik} \in \{0,1\}$ indicate whether example $i$ has label $k$. The loss sums binary cross-entropy over all labels independently:

$$E(\{\mathbf{w}_k\}) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\left[r_{ik}\ln \sigma(\mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}_i) + (1-r_{ik})\ln(1-\sigma(\mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}_i))\right]$$

Threshold Selection in Multi-label

Instead of a fixed 0.5 threshold, the operating threshold per label is often tuned on the validation set to optimise F1-score or other metrics. Common choices:

  • Global threshold: Same $\tau$ for all labels. Simple but ignores label frequency differences.
  • Per-label threshold: Choose $\tau_k$ separately for each label $k$. More flexible, handles class imbalance better.
  • Top-$m$ selection: Always predict the $m$ labels with highest probability scores.

Evaluation Metrics for Multi-label

Standard accuracy is not appropriate for multi-label (a prediction could be half-right). Common metrics:

MetricFormulaMeaning
Hamming Loss $\frac{1}{NK}\sum_{i,k}\mathbb{1}[\hat{y}_{ik}\neq r_{ik}]$ Fraction of label predictions that are wrong
Subset Accuracy $\frac{1}{N}\sum_i\mathbb{1}[\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i = \mathbf{r}_i]$ Fraction of examples where the entire label set is correct
Macro F1 Average F1 per label Treats all labels equally regardless of frequency

Summary — Section 10.7

  • Logistic discrimination is discriminative — models $P(C_k|\mathbf{x})$ directly without modelling $p(\mathbf{x})$.
  • Two-class: $P(C_1|\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x})$. Linear boundary. Cross-entropy loss. Gradient $= \sum_i(y_i - r_i)\mathbf{x}_i$. No closed form → gradient descent.
  • Multi-class: $P(C_k|\mathbf{x}) = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x})_k$. $K$ weight vectors. Categorical cross-entropy. Linear decision boundaries.
  • Multi-label: $K$ independent sigmoids. Binary cross-entropy per label. Outputs do NOT sum to 1. Evaluate with Hamming loss, F1, etc.
  • Cross-entropy loss for logistic regression is convex — gradient descent finds the global minimum.
5.1

Multivariate Data

Real-world objects are rarely described by a single measurement. A patient has blood pressure, heart rate, age, weight; an email has word frequencies, sender, length. Multivariate data means each instance is described by $d \geq 2$ features simultaneously. The central challenge is to understand the joint structure of all features together — correlations, clustering, class separation — not just each feature in isolation.

The Data Matrix

We represent $N$ instances each with $d$ features as an $N \times d$ data matrix $\mathbf{X}$:

$$\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix} x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1d} \\ x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2d} \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{N1} & x_{N2} & \cdots & x_{Nd} \end{pmatrix}$$

Row $i$ is the feature vector $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ for instance $i$. Column $j$ is the vector of all observations of feature $j$. The supervised case also has an output matrix $\mathbf{R}$ (class labels or response values).

Types of Features

TypeDescriptionExampleDistances / Operations
Numeric (continuous)Real-valued, arithmetic makes senseHeight, temperature, priceEuclidean, subtraction, mean
OrdinalOrdered categories, differences not meaningfulRating (1–5), education levelRank-based; median, not mean
Nominal (categorical)Unordered categoriesColor, city, blood typeHamming distance; mode
BinaryTwo values (0/1 or yes/no)Spam/not, purchase/notJaccard, Hamming

Most ML algorithms require numeric input. Categorical features must be encoded — typically as one-hot vectors (one binary feature per category level).

Summary Statistics for Multivariate Data

The key summary statistics extend the scalar mean and variance to vectors and matrices:

Sample Mean Vector

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$$

The $j$-th component $\bar{x}_j = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i x_{ij}$ is simply the mean of feature $j$.

Sample Covariance Matrix

$$\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$$

Diagonal entry $S_{jj} = \text{Var}(x_j)$. Off-diagonal entry $S_{jk} = \text{Cov}(x_j, x_k)$. $\mathbf{S}$ is always symmetric and positive semi-definite.

Correlation Matrix

Normalising the covariance matrix by the standard deviations gives the correlation matrix $\mathbf{R}$:

$$R_{jk} = \frac{S_{jk}}{\sqrt{S_{jj} S_{kk}}} = \text{Corr}(x_j, x_k) \in [-1, 1]$$

$R_{jj} = 1$ always. $|R_{jk}| \approx 1$ means a strong linear relationship; $R_{jk} \approx 0$ means no linear dependence.

Standardisation and Normalisation

Features often live on very different scales (e.g., age in years vs. salary in thousands). Many algorithms (k-NN, PCA, gradient descent) are scale-sensitive. Two common pre-processing steps:

Z-score Standardisation (Standardisation)

$$\tilde{x}_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j}{s_j}, \qquad s_j = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_{ij}-\bar{x}_j)^2}$$

After standardisation: feature $j$ has mean 0 and standard deviation 1; all features are on comparable scales. Essential before PCA.

Min-Max Normalisation

$$\tilde{x}_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij} - x_{j,\min}}{x_{j,\max} - x_{j,\min}} \in [0, 1]$$

Scales each feature to [0, 1]. Sensitive to outliers (which shift min/max). Useful for neural networks with bounded activations.

⚠ Leakage Warning
Always fit the scaler on the training set only, then apply the same transformation to validation and test sets. Fitting on all data leaks test statistics into training — a common source of over-optimistic results.

Mahalanobis Distance

Euclidean distance ignores correlations and different scales. The Mahalanobis distance accounts for both:

$$d_M(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \sqrt{(\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)^T \mathbf{S}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)}$$

where $\mathbf{S}$ is the sample covariance matrix. Mahalanobis distance reduces to Euclidean when $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{I}$ (unit covariance). It is scale-invariant and accounts for feature correlations — two points that are close in Euclidean space but far in a correlated direction will still be picked up as distant.

Summary — Section 5.1

  • Multivariate data: $N$ instances each described by $d$ features → data matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$.
  • Key summaries: mean vector $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$, covariance matrix $\mathbf{S}$ (symmetric, PSD), correlation matrix $\mathbf{R}$ (values in [−1,1]).
  • Standardise with z-scores before distance-based or PCA methods; fit scaler on training data only.
  • Mahalanobis distance $d_M = \sqrt{(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\mathbf{S}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})}$ handles correlations and different scales naturally.
5.2

Parameter Estimation

Given a training sample $\mathcal{X} = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_N\}$ assumed drawn i.i.d. from a parametric family $p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$, parameter estimation is the problem of estimating the unknown parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The dominant paradigm in ML is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE).

Maximum Likelihood Estimation — Principle

Choose $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ that makes the observed data most probable:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\; \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\; \prod_{i=1}^{N} p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

In practice we maximise the log-likelihood $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ (log converts product to sum; monotone so same argmax):

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\; \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

MLE for the Multivariate Gaussian

Assume $\mathbf{x}_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$. The log-likelihood is:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = -\frac{N}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}| - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}) + \text{const}$$

Setting $\partial\ell/\partial\boldsymbol{\mu} = 0$ and $\partial\ell/\partial\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = 0$ gives the closed-form MLE estimates:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbf{x}_i = \bar{\mathbf{x}} \qquad \text{(sample mean)}$$

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf{x}_i - \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})(\mathbf{x}_i - \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})^T \qquad \text{(biased sample covariance)}$$

Biased vs. Unbiased Covariance Estimator

The MLE $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ is biased: $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}] = \frac{N-1}{N}\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \neq \boldsymbol{\Sigma}$. The unbiased estimator divides by $N-1$ (Bessel's correction):

$$\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})^T, \qquad \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{S}] = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}$$

In practice: use $N$ for MLE (maximising likelihood); use $N-1$ for unbiased estimation. When $N$ is large the difference is negligible.

📐 Why N−1?
When estimating the covariance, we substitute the estimated mean $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$ for the true $\boldsymbol{\mu}$. This "uses up" one degree of freedom — the deviations $\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}}$ are not independent (they sum to zero). Dividing by $N-1$ compensates for this shrinkage.

Properties of MLE

PropertyStatement
Consistency$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MLE}} \to \boldsymbol{\theta}^*$ as $N \to \infty$ (converges to true parameter)
Asymptotic efficiencyMLE achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound as $N \to \infty$ — no unbiased estimator has lower variance
InvarianceIf $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the MLE of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, then $f(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$ is the MLE of $f(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ for any function $f$
BiasCan be biased for finite $N$ — e.g., $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{N-1}{N}\sigma^2$

Bayesian Parameter Estimation

MLE treats $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ as a fixed unknown. The Bayesian alternative treats $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ as a random variable with a prior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ encoding our beliefs before seeing data. Bayes' theorem gives the posterior:

$$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{X}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{X} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\, p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{X})} \propto \underbrace{p(\mathcal{X} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}_{\text{prior}}$$

The MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) estimate maximises the posterior:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\; \ln p(\mathcal{X}|\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \ln p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

MAP = MLE + a regularisation term (the log-prior). With a Gaussian prior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \propto e^{-\lambda\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2}$, MAP corresponds to $L_2$-regularised MLE (Ridge regression).

✦ MLE vs MAP vs Bayes
  • MLE: Maximise likelihood. No prior. Can overfit with small $N$.
  • MAP: Maximise posterior = likelihood × prior. Acts like regularised MLE. Single point estimate.
  • Full Bayes: Use full posterior distribution. Most principled but often intractable; use MCMC or variational inference.

Summary — Section 5.2

  • MLE: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \arg\max \sum_i \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i|\boldsymbol{\theta})$. Consistent, asymptotically efficient.
  • For multivariate Gaussian: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}} = \bar{\mathbf{x}}$, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum(\mathbf{x}_i-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^T$ (biased). Use $N-1$ for unbiased estimate.
  • MAP = MLE + log-prior regularisation. Gaussian prior → Ridge regression.
  • MLE can overfit for small $N$ or when $d \gg N$ (covariance not full rank). Regularisation / shrinkage helps.

Missing Values

Real-world datasets are rarely complete. A patient might skip a lab test; a survey respondent might leave fields blank; a sensor might malfunction. Missing values must be handled before applying most ML algorithms. The approach depends critically on why values are missing.

The Three Mechanisms of Missingness (Rubin 1976)

The missingness mechanism determines what analysis is valid:

MechanismAcronymDefinitionExampleSafe to ignore?
Missing Completely At Random MCAR Probability of being missing is independent of both observed and unobserved values Random equipment failure; coin flip decides which record is deleted Yes — complete-case analysis is unbiased (just loses power)
Missing At Random MAR Probability of being missing depends on observed variables but not on the missing value itself Older patients less likely to complete an online survey (age is observed) If we condition on observed vars — imputation methods are valid
Missing Not At Random MNAR Probability of being missing depends on the unobserved value itself High earners refuse to reveal income; very sick patients drop out of trial No — introduces bias; requires modelling the missingness mechanism jointly
⚠ MNAR is the Danger Zone
MNAR data cannot be handled by simple imputation — the very value that is missing is related to whether it is missing. For example, if sickest patients drop out of a clinical trial, imputing with the average of the remaining (healthier) patients will bias the analysis. This requires joint modelling of the outcome and missingness, or sensitivity analysis.

Strategies for Handling Missing Values

1. Complete-Case Analysis (Listwise Deletion)

Simply delete any row with at least one missing value. Train on the remaining complete rows.

  • Pro: Simple, no imputation assumptions needed.
  • Con: Wastes data (can lose a lot if missingness is spread across features). Biased unless MCAR.

2. Mean / Median / Mode Imputation

Replace missing value $x_{ij}$ with the mean (numeric), median (ordinal/skewed), or mode (categorical) of feature $j$ computed over the observed values.

x̃ᵢⱼ = { mean(x₁ⱼ, ..., xᵢ₋₁,ⱼ, xᵢ₊₁,ⱼ..., xₙⱼ) if x_{ij} is numeric { mode(...) if x_{ij} is categorical
  • Pro: Extremely simple, preserves all rows.
  • Con: Underestimates variance (all missing values become the same), distorts correlations between features.

3. Regression Imputation

For each feature with missing values, fit a regression model using the other features as predictors, then use it to predict the missing value:

$$\hat{x}_{ij} = f_j(\mathbf{x}_{i,{-j}})$$

where $\mathbf{x}_{i,-j}$ denotes all features of instance $i$ except feature $j$.

  • Pro: Uses information from other features; preserves correlations better than mean imputation.
  • Con: Assumes values are predictable from other features; still underestimates variance (no noise added to imputed values).

4. Multiple Imputation

Instead of a single imputed value, generate $m$ different plausible imputations by adding random noise to the regression predictions. Fit the model $m$ times on the $m$ completed datasets, then combine (pool) the results using Rubin's rules. This properly propagates uncertainty from the missing data.

5. EM Algorithm for Gaussian Data (ML Imputation)

For multivariate Gaussian data, the Expectation-Maximisation (EM) algorithm handles missing data elegantly:

  1. E-step: Given current parameters $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})$, compute the conditional expectation of missing values given the observed values for each instance: $\mathbb{E}[x_{ij}^{\text{miss}} \mid \mathbf{x}_i^{\text{obs}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}]$ using the Gaussian conditional formula.
  2. M-step: Update $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})$ using the completed data (observed + expected missing values) via the MLE formulas.
  3. Repeat E and M steps until convergence (change in parameters below a threshold).

The Gaussian conditional is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}^m \mid \mathbf{x}^o] = \boldsymbol{\mu}^m + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{mo}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{oo})^{-1}(\mathbf{x}^o - \boldsymbol{\mu}^o)$$

where superscripts $m$ and $o$ index the missing and observed features respectively.

Then impute $x_j$ (with mean or 0) and include both $x_j$ and $m_j$ as inputs. This allows the model to learn that the fact of missingness} itself is informative (common in MNAR settings).

Summary — Section 5.3

  • Three mechanisms: MCAR (random, safe to ignore), MAR (depends on observed, imputation valid), MNAR (depends on missing value itself, dangerous).
  • Listwise deletion: Simple but wastes data; biased unless MCAR.
  • Mean imputation: Destroys variance and correlations — only use as baseline.
  • Regression imputation: Preserves correlations; still underestimates variance without added noise.
  • EM imputation: Principled for Gaussian data; alternates between filling expected missing values (E-step) and updating parameters (M-step).
  • Multiple imputation: Gold standard when uncertainty propagation matters.
5.4

The Multivariate Normal Distribution

Density Function

The Multivariate Normal (MVN) or Multivariate Gaussian distribution over $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is parameterised by mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ and symmetric positive-definite covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$:

$$p(\mathbf{x}\mid\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

The term $(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})$ is the Mahalanobis distance squared between $\mathbf{x}$ and $\boldsymbol{\mu}$. Contours of constant density are ellipsoids whose axes are determined by the eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ and whose lengths scale with the eigenvalues.

Covariance Structure Types

Eigendecomposition of Σ

Since $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is symmetric positive-definite it admits $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{U}^T$ where $\mathbf{U}$ is orthogonal (eigenvectors as columns) and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}=\text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_d)$. The axes of the density ellipsoid point along the eigenvectors and have half-lengths $\propto\sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

The normalising constant requires $|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|=\prod_i \lambda_i > 0$ (from PD) and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{-1}\mathbf{U}^T$.

Marginal and Conditional Distributions

Partition $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)$ with corresponding partition of $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\\ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}\end{pmatrix}$$

Marginal: $\mathbf{x}_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11})$ — marginals of a Gaussian are Gaussian.

Conditional:

$$\mathbf{x}_1 \mid \mathbf{x}_2 \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2),\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}\right)$$

The conditional variance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}$ is the Schur complement of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}$ in $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ and is always $\preceq\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11}$ — observing $\mathbf{x}_2$ reduces uncertainty about $\mathbf{x}_1$.

Linear Transformations

If $\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ and $\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b}$, then:

$$\mathbf{y} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}+\mathbf{b},\; \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{A}^T)$$

This makes the Gaussian closed under linear transformations. Special case: the sum of independent Gaussians is Gaussian.

Key Properties of MVN
  • Uncorrelated $\Rightarrow$ independent (unique to Gaussians among continuous distributions)
  • Completely characterised by mean and covariance (first two moments suffice)
  • Maximum entropy distribution for given mean and covariance
  • MLE estimates: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}=\bar{\mathbf{x}}$, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum(\mathbf{x}_i-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^T$
5.5

Multivariate Classification

Gaussian Generative Classifiers

Model each class $C_k$ as a Gaussian: $p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$. Apply Bayes' rule for the posterior:

$$P(C_k\mid\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k)\,P(C_k)}{\sum_j p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_j)\,P(C_j)}$$

The discriminant function (log-posterior up to a constant) is:

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = \ln p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k) + \ln P(C_k) = -\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k) - \frac{d}{2}\ln 2\pi - \frac{1}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k| + \ln P(C_k)$$

Assign $\mathbf{x}$ to $\arg\max_k g_k(\mathbf{x})$.

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)

Assume all classes share the same covariance: $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\;\forall k$ (homoscedastic). Terms involving $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ cancel in $g_k$, leaving a linear function of $\mathbf{x}$:

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{x} - \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k + \ln P(C_k)$$
LDA decision boundary between C_i and C_j: (μ_i − μ_j)ᵀ Σ⁻¹ x = ½(μ_i − μ_j)ᵀ Σ⁻¹(μ_i + μ_j) − ln[P(C_i)/P(C_j)] → a hyperplane in ℝᵈ

Parameter Estimation for LDA

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_{i:y_i=k}\mathbf{x}_i, \qquad \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_k\sum_{i:y_i=k}(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)^T$$

The pooled within-class covariance uses all $N$ samples, making it more stable than per-class estimates when $N$ is small relative to $d$.

Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA)

Allow each class its own covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$ (heteroscedastic). The $-\frac{1}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k|$ and the quadratic form both survive, giving a quadratic decision boundary:

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}\mathbf{x} + \boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}\mathbf{x} - \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k - \frac{1}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k| + \ln P(C_k)$$

QDA is more flexible than LDA but requires estimating $K \times d(d+1)/2$ covariance parameters vs. $d(d+1)/2$ for LDA — much higher model complexity.

Fisher's Linear Discriminant

Fisher's approach to 2-class LDA: find the projection direction $\mathbf{w}$ that maximises class separation relative to within-class spread:

$$J(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{S}_B\mathbf{w}}{\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{S}_W\mathbf{w}}$$

where $\mathbf{S}_B = (\boldsymbol{\mu}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_2)(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_2)^T$ is the between-class scatter and $\mathbf{S}_W = \sum_k\sum_{i:y_i=k}(\mathbf{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)(\mathbf{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T$ is the within-class scatter.

Optimal projection: $\mathbf{w}^* = \mathbf{S}_W^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_2)$ — the Fisher direction. Note that when class priors are equal, Fisher LDA and Bayes LDA give identical decision rules.

5.6

Tuning Complexity of Multivariate Models

The Curse of Dimensionality

As feature dimension $d$ grows, the required sample size grows exponentially to cover the space at the same density. Specific manifestations:

  • Volume explosion: A hypersphere of radius 1 occupies a vanishing fraction of the unit hypercube as $d\to\infty$ — almost all volume is near the corners.
  • Distance concentration: Distances between random points become similar: $\frac{\max d - \min d}{\min d} \to 0$, making nearest-neighbour meaningless.
  • Covariance estimation: Estimating a full $d\times d$ covariance needs $O(d^2)$ parameters; with $N < d$ the sample covariance is singular.

Regularised Covariance Estimation

When $N \lesssim d$, the sample covariance $\mathbf{S}$ is rank-deficient. Regularisation shrinks it toward a well-conditioned target $\mathbf{T}$:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_\lambda = (1-\lambda)\mathbf{S} + \lambda\mathbf{T}, \qquad \lambda\in[0,1]$$

Common targets $\mathbf{T}$:

  • $\mathbf{T} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I}$ (identity) — Ridge covariance; shrinks toward spherical.
  • $\mathbf{T} = \bar{\sigma}^2\mathbf{I}$ where $\bar{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{d}\text{tr}(\mathbf{S})$ — mean diagonal.
  • $\mathbf{T} = \text{diag}(\mathbf{S})$ — diagonal; removes estimated correlations.

Ledoit–Wolf Shrinkage

The Ledoit–Wolf estimator finds the analytically optimal $\lambda^*$ that minimises the expected squared Frobenius error between the estimator and true $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$:

$$\lambda^* = \arg\min_\lambda \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_\lambda - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\right\|_F^2\right]$$

It has a closed-form solution in terms of $N$, $d$, and statistics of $\mathbf{S}$. It is the default robust covariance estimator in scikit-learn's LDA when $\text{solver}=\texttt{lsqr}$ with $\texttt{shrinkage}=\texttt{'auto'}$.

Reducing Dimensionality Before Classification

Feature selection: Keep the $m < d$ most informative features (filter, wrapper, or embedded methods). Reduces estimation burden and improves interpretability.

PCA pre-processing: Project onto the top-$m$ principal components of the pooled covariance. In the PC space the covariance is diagonal, so full LDA has only $m$ parameters per class mean plus $m$ variance terms.

Practical heuristic: choose m (or λ) by cross-validation on validation error. scikit-learn LDA: n_components, shrinkage, solver parameters.
Summary: Complexity Control for MVN Classifiers
  • Most constrained: Naïve Bayes — diagonal $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, $O(d)$ parameters per class.
  • Intermediate: LDA — shared full $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, $O(d^2)$ shared parameters.
  • Most flexible: QDA — per-class full $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$, $O(Kd^2)$ total.
  • Best practice: Regularise LDA/QDA with Ledoit–Wolf or cross-validated $\lambda$.
5.7

Discrete Features & Naïve Bayes

Naïve Bayes Assumption

When features are discrete (or mixed), the full joint $p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k)$ is intractable. Naïve Bayes assumes conditional independence of features given the class:

$$p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k) = \prod_{j=1}^{d} p(x_j\mid C_k)$$

This reduces parameter count from $O(V^d)$ to $O(d \cdot V)$ where $V$ is vocabulary/feature values. The classification rule becomes:

$$\hat{y} = \arg\max_k \left[\ln P(C_k) + \sum_{j=1}^d \ln p(x_j\mid C_k)\right]$$

Bernoulli Naïve Bayes

For binary features $x_j \in \{0,1\}$. Each feature is Bernoulli with parameter $\pi_{jk} = P(x_j=1\mid C_k)$:

$$p(x_j\mid C_k) = \pi_{jk}^{x_j}(1-\pi_{jk})^{1-x_j}$$

MLE: $\hat{\pi}_{jk} = \frac{\text{# examples in } C_k \text{ with } x_j=1}{N_k}$. Problem: if a feature never appears in training, $\hat{\pi}_{jk}=0$ and the whole product collapses to zero.

Multinomial Naïve Bayes

For count features (bag-of-words, histograms). Assume word counts follow a Multinomial:

$$p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k) \propto \prod_{j=1}^V \theta_{jk}^{x_j}, \qquad \theta_{jk} = P(\text{word }j\mid C_k)$$

MLE: $\hat{\theta}_{jk} = \frac{\text{count of word }j\text{ in class }k\text{ documents}}{\text{total word count in class }k}$.

Laplace (Add-α) Smoothing

Prevents zero-probability predictions by adding a pseudo-count $\alpha > 0$ (usually $\alpha=1$) to all counts:

$$\hat{\theta}_{jk} = \frac{N_{jk} + \alpha}{N_k + \alpha V}, \qquad \hat{\pi}_{jk} = \frac{N_{jk} + \alpha}{N_k + 2\alpha}$$

$\alpha=1$: Laplace smoothing. $0 < \alpha < 1$: Lidstone smoothing. This corresponds to a Dirichlet (conjugate) prior on $\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$.

Categorical Features (General)

For a feature $x_j$ taking $V_j$ values, model $p(x_j=v\mid C_k)$ with a categorical distribution of $V_j-1$ parameters per class. Laplace smoothing: add $\alpha$ to each cell.

Gaussian Naïve Bayes handles continuous features by assuming $x_j\mid C_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{jk},\sigma_{jk}^2)$ — a diagonal LDA.

Why Naïve Bayes Works Despite the Naive Assumption

Even when the independence assumption is violated, Naïve Bayes can rank classes correctly if the model is systematic in its errors. It requires only that the Bayes MAP decision is correct, not that probabilities are calibrated. In practice it is remarkably competitive for text classification.

Multivariate Regression

Multiple Linear Regression

With $d$ input features $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ and a scalar output $y\in\mathbb{R}$:

$$\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x_1 + \cdots + w_d x_d = \mathbf{w}^T\tilde{\mathbf{x}}, \quad \tilde{\mathbf{x}} = [1, x_1,\ldots,x_d]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$$

Stacking $N$ examples into design matrix $\boldsymbol{\Phi}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times(d+1)}$ (rows = augmented feature vectors):

$$\mathbf{r} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2\mathbf{I})$$

OLS minimises $\|\mathbf{r}-\boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w}\|^2$, giving the normal equations:

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{OLS}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$$

Valid when $\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}$ is invertible ($N > d$ and no multicollinearity).

Non-linear relationships are handled by lifting $\mathbf{x}$ to a polynomial feature map $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x})$ — including all monomials up to degree $p$. The number of degree-$p$ terms in $d$ variables is $\binom{d+p}{p}$, which grows rapidly:

Ridge Regression (L2 Regularisation)

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{Ridge}} = \arg\min_\mathbf{w}\left[\|\mathbf{r}-\boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|^2\right] = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}+\lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$$

Ridge adds $\lambda$ to the diagonal, making the matrix always invertible (handles $N < d$). It is the MAP estimate under a Gaussian prior $\mathbf{w}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2/\lambda\cdot\mathbf{I})$.

Lasso Regression (L1 Regularisation)

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{Lasso}} = \arg\min_\mathbf{w}\left[\|\mathbf{r}-\boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|_1\right]$$

No closed form; solved by coordinate descent. Lasso induces sparsity (exact zeros) because the L1 penalty has a kink at the origin — effectively performs feature selection.

Multiple Outputs (Multivariate Regression)

When the target is a vector $\mathbf{r}\in\mathbb{R}^q$ (multiple response variables), the model becomes $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{W}$ where $\mathbf{W}\in\mathbb{R}^{(d+1)\times q}$. Under i.i.d. Gaussian noise each column of $\mathbf{W}$ decouples, and OLS solves independently per output:

$$\hat{\mathbf{W}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\mathbf{R}$$
Regression Diagnostics Checklist
  • Residual plot: residuals vs fitted values — should be centred at 0, no pattern.
  • Q-Q plot: residuals should follow a straight line (Normality check).
  • Variance Inflation Factor (VIF): VIF$_j = 1/(1-R_j^2) > 10$ indicates multicollinearity.
  • Cook's distance: identify high-leverage / high-influence outliers.

1. Define VC dimension. What is the VC dimension of a linear classifier (hyperplane) in $\mathbb{R}^d$? [4M]

VC Dimension (2M): The VC (Vapnik–Chervonenkis) dimension of a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ is the size of the largest set of points that $\mathcal{H}$ can shatter — i.e., correctly classify in all $2^N$ possible binary labellings. Formally, $d_{VC} = \max\{N : \Pi_\mathcal{H}(N) = 2^N\}$.

Hyperplane in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (2M): A linear classifier (hyperplane through the origin with bias) in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has VC dimension $d_{VC} = d+1$. Intuition: $d+1$ points in general position can always be shattered (by choosing appropriate weight vectors), but no arrangement of $d+2$ points can always be shattered (by Radon's theorem).

2. Explain the Bias–Variance dilemma with the MSE decomposition and its implications for model selection. [4M]

Decomposition (2M): For any estimator $\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})$ of $f(\mathbf{x})$ under squared loss:

$$\text{MSE} = \underbrace{[\mathbb{E}\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})-f(\mathbf{x})]^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})-\mathbb{E}\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}))^2]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Irreducible}}$$

Implications (2M): High-complexity models (e.g., high-degree polynomial) have low bias but high variance — they overfit. Low-complexity models have high bias but low variance — they underfit. The optimal model minimises $\text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance}$. Techniques: cross-validation to select model class; regularisation to reduce variance without excessively increasing bias.

3. Derive the closed-form solution for Ridge regression and explain how it addresses the singularity problem of OLS. [4M]

Derivation (2M): Ridge minimises $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}) = \|\mathbf{r}-\boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$. Setting $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{w}=0$:

$$-2\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T(\mathbf{r}-\boldsymbol{\Phi}\mathbf{w}) + 2\lambda\mathbf{w} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}+\lambda\mathbf{I})\mathbf{w} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$$
$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{Ridge}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}+\lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\mathbf{r}$$

Addressing singularity (2M): OLS requires $\boldsymbol{\Phi}^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}$ to be invertible, which fails when $N \leq d$ (underdetermined system) or when features are collinear. Adding $\lambda\mathbf{I}$ shifts all eigenvalues up by $\lambda > 0$, guaranteeing positive definiteness and thus invertibility. Bayesian interpretation: Ridge is MAP under $\mathbf{w}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\frac{\sigma^2}{\lambda}\mathbf{I})$.

4. Explain the LDA classifier. State the assumptions, the discriminant function, and how decision boundaries are formed. [4M]

Assumptions (1M): Class-conditional densities are Gaussian, all classes share the same covariance: $p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k)=\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$.

Discriminant (2M): Applying Bayes' rule and taking logs (shared $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ terms cancel):

$$g_k(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{x} - \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_k + \ln P(C_k)$$

Assign to $\arg\max_k g_k(\mathbf{x})$. Parameters estimated as: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k = $ class mean; $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = $ pooled within-class covariance.

Boundaries (1M): The boundary between $C_i$ and $C_j$ solves $g_i(\mathbf{x})=g_j(\mathbf{x})$, which is linear in $\mathbf{x}$ — a hyperplane. With $K$ classes, there are at most $K(K-1)/2$ pairwise hyperplanes, dividing space into convex polyhedral regions.

5. Describe the three mechanisms of missing data (Rubin 1976). Which mechanism(s) allow unbiased inference from complete cases alone? [4M]

MCAR (1M): Missing Completely At Random — probability of missingness is independent of all data (observed and missing). Formally: $P(M\mid\mathbf{X}) = P(M)$. Example: sensor randomly disconnects.

MAR (1M): Missing At Random — missingness depends only on observed values, not on the missing value itself. $P(M\mid\mathbf{X}) = P(M\mid\mathbf{X}^o)$. Example: older patients less likely to report pain, age is observed.

MNAR (1M): Missing Not At Random — missingness depends on the missing value itself. $P(M\mid\mathbf{X})$ depends on $\mathbf{X}^m$. Example: high-income respondents skip income question. Requires a joint model of data and missingness process.

Unbiased inference (1M): Only MCAR guarantees that complete-case analysis is unbiased. Under MAR, likelihood-based methods (e.g., EM) that ignore the missing-data mechanism are still valid; under MNAR they are not.

6. State and explain the EM algorithm for parameter estimation with multivariate Gaussian data containing missing values. [4M]

Setup (0.5M): Partition each $\mathbf{x}_i = (\mathbf{x}_i^o, \mathbf{x}_i^m)$ into observed and missing parts. Goal: maximise the marginal log-likelihood $\sum_i \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i^o\mid\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$.

E-step (1.5M): Compute expected sufficient statistics using the current parameter estimates $(\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)},\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{(t)})$ and the Gaussian conditional formula:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_i^m\mid\mathbf{x}_i^o] = \boldsymbol{\mu}^m + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{mo}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{oo})^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_i^o - \boldsymbol{\mu}^o)$$

Also compute $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_i^m(\mathbf{x}_i^m)^T\mid\mathbf{x}_i^o]$ for the second-order statistic needed in the covariance update.

M-step (1.5M): Update parameters using the completed sufficient statistics as if all data were observed:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_i], \qquad \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_i\mathbf{x}_i^T] - \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T$$

Convergence (0.5M): Repeat until $\|\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t+1)}-\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)}\| < \epsilon$. EM is guaranteed to monotonically increase the marginal log-likelihood and converges to a (possibly local) maximum.

7. Explain how the softmax function and categorical cross-entropy loss are used in multi-class logistic regression. Write the gradient update rule. [4M]

Softmax (1M): For $K$ classes with linear scores $a_k = \mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}$, the softmax converts scores to probabilities:

$$P(C_k\mid\mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{a_k}}{\sum_{j=1}^K e^{a_j}}$$

Outputs are always positive and sum to 1. The softmax is the multi-class generalisation of the sigmoid (which equals softmax for $K=2$ up to reparameterisation).

Cross-entropy loss (1M): For $N$ examples with one-hot targets $\mathbf{r}_i$:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{i=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^K r_{ik}\ln y_{ik}, \quad y_{ik} = P(C_k\mid\mathbf{x}_i)$$

Gradient update (2M): Taking $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{w}_k$ and using the softmax Jacobian:

$$\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\mathbf{w}_k} = \sum_i(y_{ik} - r_{ik})\mathbf{x}_i$$

SGD update: $\mathbf{w}_k \leftarrow \mathbf{w}_k - \eta\sum_i(y_{ik}-r_{ik})\mathbf{x}_i$. This has the same elegant "prediction minus target" form as LMS in regression, because the softmax-cross-entropy combination is a natural exponential family (its gradient has this closed form).

8. What is the curse of dimensionality? How does it affect covariance estimation, and how can regularised LDA address this? [4M]

Curse of dimensionality (1M): As dimensionality $d$ increases, the volume of the feature space grows exponentially; a fixed-size training set becomes exponentially sparse relative to the space it must cover. Consequences: distance metrics become unreliable, density estimates require exponentially more data, model complexity grows rapidly.

Effect on covariance (1M): A full $d\times d$ covariance has $d(d+1)/2$ parameters. When $N \lesssim d$, the sample covariance $\mathbf{S}$ is rank-deficient (singular), making $\mathbf{S}^{-1}$ undefined. LDA and QDA require inverting $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ — impossible with a singular matrix.

Regularised LDA (2M): Replace $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ with a shrinkage estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_\lambda = (1-\lambda)\mathbf{S}+\lambda\mathbf{I}$, where $\lambda\in[0,1]$ is chosen by cross-validation or analytically via Ledoit–Wolf. This: (1) adds $\lambda$ to all eigenvalues, guaranteeing positive-definiteness and invertibility; (2) reduces variance of parameter estimates at the cost of some bias; (3) interpolates between full LDA ($\lambda=0$) and nearest-centroid classifier ($\lambda=1$).

9. Derive the MLE for the parameters of a Multivariate Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$. [4M]

Log-likelihood (1M):

$$\ln\mathcal{L} = -\frac{N}{2}\ln(2\pi)^d - \frac{N}{2}\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}| - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^N(\mathbf{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu})$$

MLE for $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ (1.5M): Differentiate w.r.t. $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and set to zero:

$$\frac{\partial\ln\mathcal{L}}{\partial\boldsymbol{\mu}} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\sum_i(\mathbf{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu}) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}} = \bar{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N\mathbf{x}_i$$

MLE for $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ (1.5M): Using the matrix derivative identity $\partial\ln|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|/\partial\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-T}$ and the trace trick:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})^T$$

Note: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ is biased; the unbiased estimator divides by $N-1$.

10. Explain Naïve Bayes for text classification including the conditional independence assumption, parameter estimation with Laplace smoothing, and the classification rule. [4M]

Assumption (1M): Naïve Bayes assumes that word occurrences are conditionally independent given the class label: $p(\mathbf{x}\mid C_k) = \prod_{j=1}^V p(x_j\mid C_k)$. This reduces the joint model's parameters from $O(V^d)$ to $O(V \cdot K)$.

Parameter estimation with smoothing (1.5M): For Multinomial NB, estimate word probabilities as:

$$\hat{\theta}_{jk} = \frac{N_{jk} + \alpha}{N_k + \alpha V}$$

where $N_{jk}$ = count of word $j$ in class $k$ training docs, $N_k$ = total word count in class $k$, $\alpha$ = smoothing constant (1 for Laplace). This prevents zero probabilities for unseen words at test time.

Classification rule (1.5M): For a new document $\mathbf{x}$, predict:

$$\hat{k} = \arg\max_k\left[\ln\hat{P}(C_k) + \sum_{j=1}^V x_j\ln\hat{\theta}_{jk}\right]$$

The log-sum avoids numerical underflow from multiplying many small probabilities. The class prior $\hat{P}(C_k)=N_k/N$ is also estimated from training data.

11. State and explain the PAC learning framework. Derive the sample complexity bound for a finite hypothesis class in the realizable case. [4M]

PAC Framework (1M): Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning bounds: a learner must, with probability $\geq 1-\delta$ (probably), output a hypothesis with error $\leq\varepsilon$ (approximately correct), using polynomially many examples and polynomial computation time.

Realizable case setup (1M): Assume $\exists c^* \in \mathcal{H}$ (target concept lies in the class). Any consistent hypothesis $h$ has 0 training error. We want $P(\text{error}(h) > \varepsilon) \leq \delta$.

Derivation (2M): For any "bad" hypothesis with error $> \varepsilon$, the probability it is consistent with any single training example is $\leq 1-\varepsilon$. Over $N$ examples: $P(h\text{ consistent}) \leq (1-\varepsilon)^N \leq e^{-N\varepsilon}$. Union bounding over all $|\mathcal{H}|$ hypotheses:

$$P(\exists\text{ bad consistent }h) \leq |\mathcal{H}|\cdot e^{-N\varepsilon} \leq \delta$$

Solving for $N$: $N \geq \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left(\ln|\mathcal{H}|+\ln\frac{1}{\delta}\right)$. This grows logarithmically in $|\mathcal{H}|$ — efficient learnability for large but finite hypothesis classes.

12. Compare Batch Gradient Descent, SGD, and Mini-batch GD for training a logistic regression model. Discuss trade-offs. [4M]

Batch GD (1M): Computes gradient over entire dataset per step: $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\sum_{i=1}^N\nabla\ell_i$. Pros: stable, accurate gradient direction, theoretically guaranteed convergence for convex $\mathcal{L}$. Cons: expensive per update ($O(N)$), slow for large $N$, can get stuck in flat regions.

SGD (1M): Updates on one random example at a time: $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\nabla\ell_i$. Pros: cheap per update ($O(1)$), noisy gradient can escape local minima, allows online learning. Cons: high variance in updates, oscillates around minimum, needs careful learning rate schedule.

Mini-batch GD (1M): Uses a batch of $B$ examples (typically 32–256): $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B\nabla\ell_b$. Balances pros of both: lower variance than SGD, more efficient than full Batch GD; enables GPU parallelism.

For logistic regression (1M): Since the loss is convex, Batch GD converges to the global minimum. Mini-batch SGD is preferred in practice due to computational efficiency, and with momentum/Adam, converges comparably fast. SGD with decaying learning rate is often used for very large datasets.
